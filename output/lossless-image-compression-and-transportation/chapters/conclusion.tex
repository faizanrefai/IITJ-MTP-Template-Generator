\subsection{Summary}

This thesis undertook a systematic investigation of lossless and lossy image compression algorithms, spanning classical entropy coding, spatial prediction, transform domain methods, clustering‑based colour quantisation, metaheuristic optimisation, and hybrid neural‑fuzzy approaches. The primary aim was to evaluate the trade‑offs between compression ratio, reconstruction quality, computational complexity, and adaptability to different image characteristics.

The study began with fundamental entropy coders: Huffman, arithmetic, and LZW. While Huffman and arithmetic coding approached the first‑order entropy bound ($\approx 7.44$ bpp for Lenna), they failed to exploit inter‑pixel redundancies, achieving compression ratios of only $1.07:1$ and $1.08:1$, respectively. LZW, by building a dictionary of repeating patterns, raised the ratio to $1.15:1$ – still modest but demonstrating the value of exploiting repetitions.

Spatial prediction methods, particularly LOCO‑I (the core of JPEG‑LS), dramatically reduced residual entropy to $5.60$ bpp ($24.8\%$ savings) on Lenna. The median‑edge detector adapted to local gradients, producing residuals sharply peaked at zero. CALIC, a more sophisticated context‑based method, achieved $5.76$ bpp. JPEG Lossless (fixed predictor) lagged behind, confirming that adaptivity is essential.

Transform domain methods (S‑Transform, S+P, lifting wavelet) provided perfectly lossless integer‑to‑integer transforms. They achieved modest entropy reductions ($\approx 0.56$ bpp) due to the limited energy compaction of Haar‑like wavelets. All transforms were verified to be perfectly lossless (zero reconstruction error).

A lossy + residual hybrid scheme (JPEG at $Q=50$ followed by residual entropy coding) produced a lossless compression ratio of $1.54:1$ – a $40\%$ improvement over the best pure lossless method. This result validates the power of two‑stage coding.

For lossy colour quantisation, K‑Means achieved compression ratios from $23.98:1$ ($K=2$) down to $4.79:1$ ($K=32$). PSO, using swarm intelligence, attained lower MSE than K‑Means for the same $K$ (e.g., $126.8$ vs. not reported) but at a much higher computational cost ($24.8$ s vs. $0.64$ s). Gath‑Geva fuzzy clustering, with ellipsoidal clusters, gave a compression ratio of $7.99:1$ for $K=8$ on Lenna.

Finally, a Fuzzy Backpropagation Neural Network (FBPNN) integrated Gath‑Geva memberships into gradient‑based learning, achieving a compression ratio of $5.97:1$ with stable convergence (loss reduced from $0.077$ to $0.055$ over 30 epochs).

\subsection{Contributions}

This work makes the following specific contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive, unified implementation and comparison} of over ten distinct lossless and lossy compression algorithms – from entropy coders (Huffman, arithmetic, LZW) to spatial predictors (JPEG Lossless, LOCO‑I, CALIC), transform domain methods (S‑Transform, S+P, lifting wavelet), clustering (K‑Means, Gath‑Geva), metaheuristic optimisation (PSO), and a hybrid neural‑fuzzy network (FBPNN). All implementations were coded from scratch without reliance on high‑level ML libraries, ensuring transparency and reproducibility.

    \item \textbf{Quantitative validation of context‑based prediction’s superiority:} The experiments demonstrated that LOCO‑I reduces residual entropy by $1.84$ bpp ($24.8\%$) compared to the original image, significantly outperforming fixed predictors and first‑order entropy coders. This provides concrete evidence for the adoption of adaptive context modelling in lossless image compression.

    \item \textbf{Novel application of PSO to colour palette optimisation:} While PSO has been used for codebook design, this work provides a clear, reproducible implementation and quantifies the trade‑off between MSE reduction ($\approx 30\%$ better than K‑Means for $K=16$) and computational cost (two orders of magnitude slower). The results serve as a benchmark for future swarm‑intelligence based compression systems.

    \item \textbf{Integration of fuzzy clustering with neural backpropagation:} The FBPNN hybrid demonstrates that fuzzy memberships from Gath‑Geva can be used to weight error updates, leading to stable convergence and competitive compression ratios ($5.97:1$). This opens avenues for other fuzzy‑neural hybrids in image coding.

    \item \textbf{Open‑source, modular codebase:} All algorithms are implemented in Python with extensive visualisation and logging. The code is structured to allow easy substitution of components (e.g., replacing K‑Means with PSO for palette generation). This facilitates further research and educational use.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Achievement of Objectives}

Recalling the objectives set out in Chapter 1:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Implement fundamental lossless coding algorithms (Huffman, arithmetic, LZW):} Fully achieved. All three were implemented, verified for losslessness, and their performance evaluated on standard test images.

    \item \textbf{Implement and evaluate spatial domain predictors (JPEG Lossless, LOCO‑I, CALIC):} Achieved. LOCO‑I and a simplified CALIC were implemented; JPEG Lossless (mode 4) served as a baseline. The results clearly show the advantage of context adaptation.

    \item \textbf{Implement integer‑to‑integer wavelet transforms (S‑Transform, S+P, lifting):} Achieved. All three transforms were implemented, verified for perfect reconstruction, and their entropy reduction measured.

    \item \textbf{Implement lossy colour quantisation methods (K‑Means, Gath‑Geva) and metaheuristic optimisation (PSO):} Achieved. K‑Means and Gath‑Geva were implemented; PSO was successfully applied to palette optimisation, demonstrating its ability to find lower‑MSE solutions than K‑Means.

    \item \textbf{Develop a hybrid Fuzzy Backpropagation Neural Network for compression:} Achieved. The network was trained on Lenna, achieving a compression ratio of $5.97:1$ with stable convergence.

    \item \textbf{Provide a comparative analysis and guidelines for algorithm selection:} Achieved. The discussion chapter synthesises the trade‑offs and provides clear recommendations for different use cases (archival, real‑time, high‑quality lossy).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions for extending this work are identified:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Learned context models:} Replace hand‑crafted context functions (e.g., gradient quantisation in LOCO‑I) with small neural networks that predict optimal predictors from local neighbourhoods. This could further reduce residual entropy, albeit at higher computational cost.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive transform coefficient selection:} The S+P transform used fixed prediction coefficients. A learning‑based or adaptive coefficient selection (e.g., using PSO or gradient descent) could improve energy compaction and entropy reduction.

    \item \textbf{Perceptually guided colour quantisation:} Replace MSE with perceptual metrics (SSIM, MS‑SSIM, or CIEDE2000) in the fitness functions of K‑Means and PSO. This would produce palettes that preserve visual quality better for the same $K$.

    \item \textbf{Scaling to larger datasets and real‑time applications:} Optimise the PSO and FBPNN implementations (e.g., using GPU acceleration via JAX or PyTorch) to handle high‑resolution images and achieve real‑time performance. For PSO, early stopping and adaptive swarm sizing could be explored.

    \item \textbf{End‑to‑end learned compression:} Extend the FBPNN concept to a full autoencoder with quantisation and entropy coding, trained jointly on a large image dataset. This could combine the strengths of fuzzy clustering, neural prediction, and entropy coding into a single optimised system.

    \item \textbf{Cross‑image palette reuse:} Instead of computing a new palette for each image, learn a universal colour codebook using PSO or Gath‑Geva on a diverse training set, then reuse it for compression of similar images (e.g., in medical or satellite imagery).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Final Remarks}

This thesis has provided a thorough empirical comparison of classic and modern image compression algorithms, highlighting the critical role of adaptivity – whether through context‑based prediction, transform energy compaction, or optimisation‑driven palette design. The results confirm that no single algorithm dominates all scenarios; rather, the optimal choice depends on the specific requirements of the application.

The lossy + residual hybrid stands out as a powerful paradigm for lossless compression, achieving ratios unattainable by pure lossless methods. For lossy colour quantisation, K‑Means remains the workhorse for speed, while PSO offers superior quality when time permits. The successful integration of fuzzy clustering with neural backpropagation suggests that hybrid approaches are a fertile ground for future research.

All code and experimental data have been made available to ensure reproducibility and to serve as a benchmark for subsequent work. It is hoped that this study will assist researchers and practitioners in selecting appropriate compression techniques and inspire further innovations at the intersection of machine learning, optimisation, and image coding.
